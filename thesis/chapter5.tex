\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}

\section{Summary of Contributions}

This thesis trained and evaluated four deep learning architectures---EfficientNet-B0, ResNet50, DenseNet121, and Swin-Tiny---on the ISIC 2018 seven-class skin lesion dataset, with a training pipeline built specifically around the dataset's 58:1 class imbalance. The pipeline's four components each address the imbalance from a different angle: Weighted Random Sampling at the data-loading level, Mixup and CutMix at the training-signal level, label smoothing at the target level, and cosine-annealed AdamW with weight decay at the optimisation level. Together they eliminate the majority-class collapse that appears in naive training, as confirmed by the ablation study.

At the single representative seed (42) reported in the headline tables, EfficientNet-B0 (5.3M parameters) outperformed ResNet50 (25.6M), DenseNet121 (8M), and Swin-Tiny (28M) on every hard-classification metric. When the experiment was repeated across five random seeds, however, Swin-Tiny emerged as the strongest individual backbone on average ($88.33 \pm 1.96\%$ accuracy versus EfficientNet-B0's $87.31 \pm 1.32\%$), with the seed-42 ranking reversing because of Swin-Tiny's higher variance. The lesson is the same: when training data is limited, architectural efficiency and training methodology matter more than raw parameter count, but a single-seed comparison is not enough to rank closely-matched architectures. The soft-voting ensemble showed that the four architectures are complementary rather than redundant---combining them raised macro AUC from 0.952 to 0.980 and melanoma F1-score from 0.695 to 0.744 at the headline seed, and held a $89.93 \pm 0.66\%$ accuracy across the five seeds, with McNemar's test confirming the gain over every single model is statistically significant ($p < 10^{-7}$ against the strongest baseline). The improvement comes from architectural diversity rather than additional training cost.

Grad-CAM visualisations confirmed that every model attends to the lesion interior and boundary, not to background skin or imaging artefacts. This is a necessary condition for clinical deployment---a model that achieves 88\% accuracy by attending to ruler markings is not useful, regardless of its test-set numbers. An interactive Streamlit prototype embedding real-time ensemble prediction and Grad-CAM overlay demonstrates that these models can be integrated into a functional diagnostic support interface.

Beyond the image-only baseline, two further experiments were conducted to extend and probe the result. Late-fusing the HAM10000 patient metadata vector (age, sex, anatomical location) into each backbone through a small MLP encoder pushes the four-model ensemble to 90.49\% accuracy and 0.873 macro F1, improving on Pacheco and Krohling's reported 89.0\% / 0.83 and crossing the 90\% threshold often cited as a reference point for image-based skin classification. To bound expectations honestly, the image-only ensemble was also evaluated zero-shot on PAD-UFES-20, a public dataset of 2,298 clinical smartphone photographs from a different acquisition modality. Accuracy drops to 32.5\%, a 56-percentage-point fall that is consistent with literature reports for dermoscopy-to-clinical-photo transfer without domain adaptation. Per-class AUC remains in the 0.61--0.81 range, confirming the model is not random on smartphone photographs but that hard-decision performance requires modality-specific adaptation or fine-tuning to be clinically usable.

\section{Key Findings}

Three findings from this work have implications beyond the specific dataset and architectures studied.

The accuracy--AUC inversion across models is the most practically relevant. CNNs (EfficientNet-B0 in particular) produce sharper hard decisions while Swin-Tiny produces better-calibrated probability distributions, and this difference is not just a matter of scale---it persists after identical training on identical data. The implication for system design is that the appropriate model depends on what the downstream clinical task actually requires. A binary flag for immediate referral calls for EfficientNet-B0's high F1. A ranked worklist for a screening programme calls for Swin-Tiny's superior AUC. Choosing based on either metric alone, without understanding the deployment context, risks optimising the wrong objective.

The label smoothing result from the ablation study is counterintuitive enough to warrant separate attention. Removing label smoothing simultaneously increased AUC (from 0.952 to 0.967) and decreased macro F1 (by 3.1 percentage points). Label smoothing trades calibration sharpness for decision boundary quality---it prevents confident predictions that would raise AUC but helps the model generalise the ambiguous boundaries between MEL, NV, and BKL. The conventional advice to apply label smoothing universally is therefore too coarse; the right choice depends on whether the model's output will be thresholded or used as a continuous score.

Melanoma remains the hardest class after all interventions: the ensemble's MEL F1-score of 0.744 is the lowest among all well-represented classes, even after combining four architectures with complementary inductive biases. This is not a failure of the training pipeline. It reflects the genuine clinical difficulty of separating early-stage melanoma from benign nevi---a task that experienced dermatologists also find unreliable---and suggests that further progress on this specific class requires either melanoma-targeted feature learning, segmentation supervision for lesion boundaries, or substantially more MEL training data.

\section{Limitations}

Several constraints limit the conclusions that can be drawn from this study:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Single dataset.} All training and evaluation was conducted on ISIC 2018. The trained models have not been tested on ISIC 2019/2020 or external clinical cohorts, so their generalisation to different imaging equipment or patient populations is unknown.
    \item \textbf{Fixed $224 \times 224$ resolution.} Higher resolutions---which architectures such as EfficientNetV2~\cite{tan2021efficientnetv2} are designed to exploit---may recover discriminative features lost at the standard resolution, particularly for subtle pigmentation patterns.
    \item \textbf{Small rare-class test sets.} The test set contains 17 Dermatofibroma and 22 Vascular Lesion samples. A single additional misclassification shifts the DF F1-score by approximately 0.06, so per-class figures for these classes carry high variance and should not be over-interpreted.
    \item \textbf{No clinical validation.} The models were not evaluated by practising dermatologists within a patient-facing workflow. Human--AI comparative studies and clinician acceptance assessments are necessary prerequisites for any deployment consideration.
    \item \textbf{No statistical significance testing.} All comparisons are based on point estimates from a single stratified train/test split. Bootstrap confidence intervals or McNemar's test would provide stronger evidence for the observed performance differences between models.
    \item \textbf{Single Transformer architecture.} Only Swin-Tiny was evaluated. Other Transformer variants (DeiT~\cite{touvron2021deit}, ConvNeXt~\cite{liu2022convnext}, or larger Swin variants) may produce different accuracy--AUC trade-offs.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Work}

The most direct extensions of this work address its main limitations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Test-time augmentation (TTA).} Averaging predictions over multiple augmented versions of each test image is straightforward to implement and typically gains 1--2 percentage points of accuracy without retraining. A TTA module was implemented in the project codebase but excluded from the reported results to ensure consistent comparison conditions.
    \item \textbf{Per-class threshold tuning.} The default 0.5 decision threshold is suboptimal for imbalanced multi-class problems. Tuning per-class thresholds on the validation ROC curves can increase sensitivity for melanoma at a controlled cost to specificity, directly addressing the MEL F1 weakness identified in this study.
    \item \textbf{Cross-dataset evaluation.} Applying the trained models to ISIC 2019/2020 or private clinical datasets would test whether the regularisation strategy generalises or is tuned to the HAM10000 distribution~\cite{tschandl2018ham10000}.
    \item \textbf{Self-supervised pretraining.} Methods such as DINO~\cite{caron2021dino} can extract strong visual representations from large unlabeled dermoscopy collections before fine-tuning on labeled data, potentially reducing the effective data requirement and improving minority-class generalisation.
    \item \textbf{Segmentation-assisted classification.} Jointly predicting lesion boundaries~\cite{hasan2020dsnet} alongside the disease label would give the model explicit spatial supervision for the boundary features that are most relevant to the MEL/NV distinction.
    \item \textbf{Knowledge distillation.} Compressing the four-model ensemble into a single lightweight model would preserve most of the accuracy gain while reducing inference cost to that of a single forward pass---a practical requirement for mobile or point-of-care deployment.
    \item \textbf{Formal calibration analysis.} Applying temperature scaling and plotting reliability diagrams would formally quantify whether predicted probabilities match observed class frequencies, a standard requirement for clinical decision support systems where the probability output is consumed directly.
\end{itemize}
