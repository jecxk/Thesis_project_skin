\chapter{Experiments and Results}

All experiments were conducted on a single NVIDIA GPU using mixed-precision (FP16) training with PyTorch~2.x. The random seed was fixed at 42 throughout. Results are reported on the held-out test set, which no model saw during training or hyperparameter selection.

\section{Individual Model Comparison}

\subsection{Overall Performance}

Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} summarises test-set performance for all four architectures trained with the optimised pipeline.

\begin{table}[H]

\begin{tabular}{lccccc}

\textbf{Model} & \textbf{Accuracy} & \textbf{Macro F1} & \textbf{AUC} & \textbf{MCC} & \textbf{Kappa} \\
\midrule
EfficientNet-B0 & \textbf{0.8836} & \textbf{0.8311} & 0.9522 & \textbf{0.7721} & \textbf{0.7710} \\
ResNet50        & 0.8603 & 0.7902 & 0.9565 & 0.7310 & 0.7309 \\
DenseNet121     & 0.8543 & 0.7967 & 0.9587 & 0.7209 & 0.7206 \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

EfficientNet-B0 leads on every hard-classification metric despite having fewer parameters than any other model: 5.3M versus 8M (DenseNet121), 25.6M (ResNet50), and 28M (Swin-Tiny). Its macro F1-score of 0.831 exceeds ResNet50's 0.790 by 4.1 percentage points, despite the latter having nearly five times as many parameters. This is consistent with the compound-scaling hypothesis of Tan and Le~\cite{tan2019efficientnet}: jointly optimising depth, width, and input resolution produces a qualitatively different efficiency frontier rather than marginal incremental gains.\footnote{The relatively high test cross-entropy loss (0.75) observed for all models is an expected consequence of label smoothing~\cite{muller2019does}: models trained with soft targets ($\epsilon=0.1$) output a maximum confidence of approximately $1 - \epsilon + \epsilon/C \approx 0.93$ rather than 1.0, producing higher raw loss values that do not reflect any degradation in classification quality.}

One pattern stands out when comparing accuracy against AUC across models: they invert. EfficientNet-B0 leads on hard-classification metrics while Swin-Tiny, which trails by 3.5 percentage points in accuracy, produces the highest AUC (0.960). The Transformer's window-based self-attention appears to generate better-calibrated probability distributions even when its hard predictions are less accurate---plausibly because global context integration blends evidence across the entire lesion, smoothing confidence estimates toward the true class frequencies. This calibration advantage does not translate to better discrete decisions on 7,010 training images, which is consistent with the finding that Vision Transformers require substantially more data to close the gap with CNNs on hard classification~\cite{touvron2021deit}. In a clinical triage application where the model's probability output is consumed directly for risk ranking rather than binary decision-making, the AUC ordering would be the relevant comparison.

Melanoma (MEL) is the hardest class for every architecture, with F1-scores ranging from 0.625 (Swin-Tiny) to 0.695 (EfficientNet-B0). This difficulty is structural: MEL overlaps with BKL in pigmentation and with NV in texture, the same ambiguity that makes melanoma challenging to diagnose clinically. The AUC values tell a more encouraging story---0.913 to 0.930---indicating that the models consistently rank true melanoma cases above benign alternatives even when the hard 0.5 threshold fails to separate them. In a triage workflow where cases are sorted by risk score rather than flagged by a binary cutoff, this probability ordering is the output that matters.

Dermatofibroma (DF) sits at the opposite extreme: F1-scores of 0.800--0.938 despite having only 81 training and 17 test samples. The Weighted Random Sampler ensures that Dermatofibroma appears in every mini-batch rather than being swamped by NV, and DF's distinct clinical appearance---a firm, button-like lesion with characteristic dermoscopic features---provides a clear discriminative signal. However, these figures carry high variance: with 17 test samples, a single additional misclassification shifts the F1-score by approximately 0.06, so the apparent 14-point gap between EfficientNet-B0 (0.938) and ResNet50 (0.800) should not be read as a reliable performance difference.

Vascular Lesion (VASC) is consistently well-classified, with AUC reaching 1.000 for two architectures. The red-purple coloration of vascular lesions provides a strong, unambiguous colour signal that all models pick up reliably. As with DF, the 22-sample test set means individual figures are noisy, but the near-perfect AUC values are stable across architectures and reflect the visual clarity of this class.

\section{Statistical Robustness Across Random Seeds}

The numbers reported above were obtained at a single random seed (42). To verify that the ensemble's apparent advantage is not an artefact of one fortunate initialisation, the entire training and ensembling pipeline was repeated with five independent seeds (\{42, 1337, 2024, 7, 11\}). With CuDNN's non-deterministic kernels and the stochastic Mixup/CutMix sampling, the seed perturbs the entire optimisation trajectory, so the five runs provide an empirical estimate of variance attributable to randomness rather than to methodology. Table~\ref{tab:multiseed} reports the mean and standard deviation across the five seeds for each backbone and for the ensemble.

Two observations are worth emphasising. First, the single-seed headline result of 89.49\% accuracy reported in Table~\ref{tab:ensemble_results} lies within one standard deviation of the multi-seed mean of 89.93\%, confirming that it represents typical rather than fortunate behaviour. The ensemble's accuracy standard deviation of 0.66 percentage points is substantially smaller than that of any individual backbone (0.57--2.42 points), and its macro AUC variance is essentially negligible at $\pm 0.001$. This is the expected behaviour for a soft-voting ensemble: probability averaging across architecturally diverse models absorbs the seed-level variance that any single network exhibits, producing a more stable estimator at the cost of a small amount of bias.

Second, Swin-Tiny's multi-seed mean accuracy of 88.33\% reveals it as the strongest individual backbone---not EfficientNet-B0 as the seed-42 ranking suggested. The seed-42 Swin run early-stopped on an unusually unfavourable validation point, producing the 84.90\% figure. Averaging across seeds shows that Swin-Tiny's global self-attention is in fact competitive with the convolutional architectures on this task; its weakness is variance, not mean performance. This argues retrospectively for keeping Swin-Tiny in the ensemble: the very property that hurts its single-model standing (higher variance) is exactly what makes it valuable as an ensemble component, since its errors are less correlated with those of the CNN models.

\section{Patient Metadata Fusion}

The image-only pipeline ignores three patient attributes that any practising dermatologist would consider before committing to a diagnosis: the patient's age, sex, and the anatomical location of the lesion. A lesion on the back of a 30-year-old has different prior probabilities of being benign or malignant than the same dermoscopic image taken from the face of a 75-year-old. The HAM10000 release includes these three attributes for every image, but the original ISIC 2018 Task 3 split distributed only the image-class labels. After downloading the full \texttt{HAM10000\_metadata.csv} file and merging it onto the project split, each image is associated with a 19-dimensional metadata vector: one standardised age value, a three-way one-hot encoding of sex (\texttt{male}, \texttt{female}, \texttt{unknown}), and a fifteen-way one-hot encoding of anatomical localization. Missing ages were imputed with the dataset mean (51.86~years), and missing sex/localization were coded as the explicit \texttt{unknown} category.

A late-fusion strategy following Pacheco and Krohling~\cite{pacheco2020metadata} integrates this vector with each backbone. The image stream remains unchanged: the same four backbones used throughout this work pass through their convolutional or attention stages and emerge as a pooled feature vector. The metadata vector is processed in parallel by a small two-layer MLP encoder ($19 \rightarrow 64 \rightarrow 32$ with ReLU activations and a 0.1 dropout after the hidden layer), producing a 32-dimensional learned representation. The image feature and the metadata feature are concatenated and passed through a single linear classifier producing the seven-class logits. Training reuses the entire pipeline described earlier (Weighted Random Sampling, Mixup, CutMix, label smoothing, AdamW with cosine annealing); only the model definition and the dataset loader differ. Table~\ref{tab:meta_fusion} reports the headline test-set results for each of the four backbones with metadata fusion and for the resulting four-model soft-voting ensemble.

Three observations stand out. First, metadata fusion is not uniformly beneficial at the single-model level: EfficientNet-B0 \textit{loses} 4.6 percentage points of accuracy when forced to accommodate the metadata MLP, almost certainly because its small parameter budget (5.3M) makes the marginal cost of adding even a 3.4K-parameter encoder visible in the optimisation. The three larger backbones all gain. Second, the gain is dramatic for Swin-Tiny: the metadata-fused Swin-Tiny single model reaches 89.82\% accuracy and 0.871 macro F1, on its own surpassing the four-model image-only ensemble. Transformers appear to integrate auxiliary features through their self-attention more effectively than CNNs in this setting. Third, the ensemble of all four metadata-fused models recovers the variance reduction observed in the image-only setting and pushes the headline accuracy to 90.49\% with macro F1 0.873 and macro AUC 0.984. This result improves on Pacheco and Krohling's reported 89.0\% / 0.83 and represents, to the author's knowledge, the strongest image+metadata fusion result published on the ISIC 2018 Task 3 test set.

\section{Out-of-Distribution Evaluation on Clinical Smartphone Photographs}

A model trained on dermoscopic images is not the same product as a model deployable on smartphone-acquired clinical photographs. ISIC 2018 was collected with polarised-light dermoscopes, which produce a characteristic illumination, magnification, and contrast profile that is absent from a phone camera held above a skin lesion at arm's length. To quantify the gap, the image-only ensemble was evaluated zero-shot on the PAD-UFES-20 dataset, a publicly available collection of 2,298 clinical skin-lesion photographs taken with smartphones by the Universidade Federal do Espírito Santo Dermatological and Surgical Assistance Program~\cite{pacheco2020pad}. PAD-UFES-20 covers six diagnostic classes that map onto five of the seven ISIC 2018 classes: \texttt{ACK} and \texttt{SCC} both map to \texttt{akiec} following Pacheco's own labelling convention, \texttt{BCC} maps to \texttt{bcc}, \texttt{SEK} maps to \texttt{bkl}, \texttt{NEV} maps to \texttt{nv}, and \texttt{MEL} maps to \texttt{mel}. Dermatofibroma and vascular lesions have no PAD-UFES counterpart and are simply absent from the OOD test. No domain adaptation, no fine-tuning, no test-time training: the ensemble that produced 88.9\% accuracy on ISIC 2018 is loaded as-is and run on PAD-UFES-20. Table~\ref{tab:ood_pad_ufes} reports the result.

The accuracy drop from 88.9\% to 32.5\%---a 56-percentage-point fall---looks catastrophic in isolation, but it is consistent with what the dermoscopy-to-clinical literature reports for zero-shot transfer without any adaptation~\cite{pacheco2020metadata}. Three observations are more diagnostic than the headline number. The first is that the per-class AUC stays between 0.61 and 0.81: the model is not random on PAD-UFES, its probability rankings retain enough signal that hard decisions degrade more than soft decisions. The second is that NV recall is 0.72: the model defaults toward predicting NV (its dominant ISIC training class at 67\% of the training set), a classical prior-collapse failure mode whenever a classifier is forced to extrapolate. The third is the inversion of the AKIEC and MEL behaviour: AKIEC has the highest precision (0.54) and the lowest recall (0.14), so when the model does call AKIEC it is usually correct but it misses most actual cases, whereas MEL is precisely the opposite (precision 0.06, recall 0.31): the model over-calls melanoma on smartphone photographs but rarely catches the actual ones. Neither of those error profiles is acceptable for direct clinical deployment without further work.

The honest conclusion is that the present pipeline is not ready to be pointed at consumer-grade dermatology photographs. Closing this gap is not a research mystery, only an engineering exercise: domain adaptation, fine-tuning on a labelled clinical-photograph collection, or even a simple style-transfer pre-processing step would all push the OOD numbers materially closer to the in-distribution ones. Reporting the OOD result here serves to bound expectations and to flag the modality gap as the single most important constraint on real-world deployment of any ISIC-trained classifier.

\section{Grad-CAM Interpretability Analysis}

To verify that models attend to clinically relevant image regions rather than spurious correlates (e.g.\ hair, rulers, ink markings), Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM)~\cite{selvaraju2017grad} was applied to all four architectures on held-out test images. For each CNN the gradient signal was captured at the last convolutional feature map before global average pooling; for Swin-Tiny, at the final window-attention block's layer-normalisation output. Figure~\ref{fig:gradcam_grid} presents a systematic comparison across all seven disease classes.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/gradcam_class_grid.png}
\caption{Grad-CAM heatmaps for one representative test image per class (rows) and all four models (columns). Warm colours (red/yellow) indicate high gradient-weighted activation; cool colours (blue) indicate low activation. Prediction labels are shown above each heatmap; green denotes a correct prediction and red denotes a misclassification.}
\label{fig:gradcam_grid}
\end{figure}

The Grad-CAM analysis reveals several consistent and architecture-specific patterns:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{All models localise attention within the lesion boundary.} Across all seven classes, the highest-activation regions fall inside or immediately adjacent to the lesion, with negligible response to surrounding healthy skin, background, or dermoscopic artefacts. The models have learned to ignore the image periphery and focus on the tissue region, which is a necessary condition for clinical trustworthiness.

    \item \textbf{CNN models produce compact, lesion-centred hotspots.} EfficientNet-B0, ResNet50, and DenseNet121 all generate heatmaps with well-defined peaks concentrated on the most discriminative sub-regions of the lesion. The locality bias of convolutional kernels naturally encourages spatially precise attention---the network must attend to something specific to justify a confident prediction.

    \item \textbf{Swin-Tiny produces broader, distributed attention patterns.} Window-based self-attention integrates evidence across larger spatial extents simultaneously, producing heatmaps that cover more of the lesion area. This broader coverage reflects the model's capacity for long-range dependency modelling, though it also means the heatmaps are less useful for highlighting a specific discriminative sub-region.

    \item \textbf{Melanoma visualisations align with ABCDE clinical criteria.} As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:gradcam_mel}, all four models attend to regions of asymmetric pigmentation, irregular borders, and multi-tonal colour variation---the same visual cues used by dermatologists when applying the ABCDE rule (Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Colour variation, Diameter, Evolution)~\cite{abbasi2004early}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Ablation Study}

To quantify the contribution of each pipeline component, an ablation study was conducted using EfficientNet-B0 as the base model. In each experiment, one component was removed while keeping all others fixed.

Removing Mixup and CutMix causes the sharpest performance drop in this study: 4.5 percentage points in macro F1. Without label-mixed training examples, the model can memorise minority-class samples---Dermatofibroma has just 81 training images, few enough to overfit within a handful of epochs---and the resulting features do not generalise to the test set. The accuracy drop is smaller (2.5 percentage points) because the majority class NV remains well-classified even without mixing, keeping overall accuracy artificially high.

Removing label smoothing causes a 3.1-point F1 drop, but simultaneously increases AUC from 0.952 to 0.967. This trade-off is real, not a measurement artefact: label smoothing improves discrete decision boundaries at the cost of probability sharpness. It prevents the model from assigning probability 1.0 to any class, which helps the model generalise the boundaries between ambiguous classes (MEL/NV/BKL) but slightly reduces the absolute confidence gap between correct and incorrect predictions, lowering AUC. Whether to apply label smoothing therefore depends on the downstream use: hard classification benefits from it; continuous risk scoring may not.

The Weighted Random Sampler contributes a 2.7-point F1 improvement. Without it, the model spends most of its learning capacity on NV, which dominates the training distribution. The drop is concentrated in minority-class recall; overall accuracy falls by only 0.7 percentage points because NV, the dominant test class, remains well-classified.

Removing advanced augmentations (RandomResizedCrop, Gaussian Blur, Random Erasing) produces the counterintuitive result of higher accuracy (88.96\%) but lower macro F1 (0.820). Aggressive augmentation makes training harder for all classes, including NV, which slightly lowers overall accuracy. At the same time, the added diversity forces the model to learn more generalisable minority-class features, improving balanced F1. This is the expected behaviour of strong augmentation on imbalanced data~\cite{shorten2019survey}: it transfers capacity from the easy majority class to the harder minority classes, a trade-off that the macro F1 metric captures but raw accuracy conceals.
