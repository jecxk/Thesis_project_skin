\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Melanoma accounts for the majority of skin cancer deaths, yet carries a five-year survival rate above 99\% when caught early. Translating that clinical fact into an automated tool requires not just a capable model but a training procedure that can handle the severe class imbalance typical of dermoscopy datasets: in the ISIC 2018 benchmark, the dominant Melanocytic Nevus class outnumbers the rarest class, Dermatofibroma, by nearly 60 to 1.

This thesis evaluates four deep learning architectures---EfficientNet-B0, ResNet50, DenseNet121, and Swin-Tiny---under a shared training pipeline designed to counter that imbalance. The pipeline combines Weighted Random Sampling, Mixup and CutMix augmentation, label smoothing, and cosine-annealed AdamW optimisation. Among the four models, EfficientNet-B0 produced the best individual results at a single random seed (88.36\% accuracy, macro F1 0.831), despite having fewer parameters than any competing architecture. A soft-voting ensemble of all four networks improved performance to 89.49\% accuracy and 0.980 macro AUC at the headline seed; repeating the full training-and-ensembling pipeline over five seeds confirms the result is statistically robust, with the ensemble averaging $89.93 \pm 0.66\%$ accuracy, $0.852 \pm 0.010$ macro F1 and $0.982 \pm 0.001$ macro AUC. McNemar's test against each backbone, combined across seeds using Fisher's method, shows the ensemble's advantage is statistically significant in every case ($p < 10^{-7}$ against the strongest single model). Late-fusing per-image HAM10000 patient metadata (age, sex, anatomical location) through an MLP encoder pushes the four-model ensemble to \textbf{90.49\% accuracy and 0.873 macro F1}, the strongest image+metadata fusion result published on ISIC 2018 to the author's knowledge. To bound expectations under domain shift, the image-only ensemble is also evaluated zero-shot on PAD-UFES-20, a publicly available collection of 2,298 clinical smartphone photographs; accuracy drops to 32.5\%, the standard cost of dermoscopy-to-clinical-photo transfer without adaptation, with per-class AUC retaining 0.61--0.81. Grad-CAM visualisations confirmed that every model focuses on the lesion region rather than surrounding skin or imaging artefacts. An ablation study identified Mixup and CutMix as the most critical training component, with their removal causing a 4.5-percentage-point drop in macro F1.

The work shows that a carefully regularised pipeline can close much of the accuracy gap between lightweight and large-capacity architectures on a limited medical dataset, and that a simple multi-model ensemble is an effective and practical strategy for multi-class skin lesion diagnosis.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

Skin cancer is globally recognized as one of the most prevalent and life-threatening forms of cancer. According to the World Health Organization~\cite{who2023skin}, the incidence of both non-melanoma and melanoma skin cancers has been increasing steadily over the past decades, with an estimated 2--3 million non-melanoma and over 130,000 melanoma cases diagnosed globally each year. Melanoma, while representing only a small fraction of skin cancer cases, is responsible for the vast majority of skin cancer-related deaths due to its rapid metastasis. However, if detected and treated in its early stages, the five-year survival rate for melanoma patients exceeds 99\%.

Dermoscopy is a non-invasive imaging technique that magnifies the surface of the skin, allowing dermatologists to observe morphological structures invisible to the naked eye. While dermoscopy significantly improves diagnostic accuracy compared to standard clinical evaluation, interpreting these images remains highly subjective and requires extensive specialized training. The visual similarities between benign lesions (such as melanocytic nevi) and malignant ones (such as melanoma) often lead to misdiagnoses, even among experienced practitioners.

Deep learning has become the dominant approach for automated lesion classification. CNNs were the first to demonstrate dermatologist-level performance on dermoscopy images~\cite{esteva2017dermatologist,maron2019systematic}, and more recently Vision Transformers~\cite{dosovitskiy2021image} have brought global context modelling to the same problem. The practical obstacle, however, is not architectural capability but data quality: the ISIC 2018 dataset~\cite{tschandl2018ham10000}, the standard benchmark for this task, is dominated by the Melanocytic Nevus class to the point where naive training consistently produces classifiers that predict the majority class for most ambiguous inputs, inflating reported accuracy while leaving minority disease categories---including melanoma---poorly detected~\cite{buda2018systematic}.

\section{Problem Statement}

The two defining problems in automated skin lesion classification are imbalance and interpretability. A model trained without intervention on ISIC 2018 learns to predict Melanocytic Nevus with overwhelming regularity: it achieves roughly 67\% accuracy by doing almost nothing useful. Recovering genuine sensitivity across all seven classes, especially melanoma and the rarer benign categories, requires deliberate correction throughout the training pipeline.

Interpretability is equally important. A black-box model that correctly flags a lesion as melanoma provides limited clinical value if the clinician cannot verify that the decision was based on the lesion itself rather than, say, a ruler marking at the image border. In a safety-critical domain, the reasoning behind a prediction must be examinable.

This thesis addresses both problems: it builds and evaluates a training pipeline that explicitly counters class imbalance, applies Grad-CAM to confirm that the learned representations are clinically grounded, and isolates through ablation which pipeline components matter most.

\section{Objectives}

To address the aforementioned challenges, this thesis outlines the following key objectives:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Develop an Optimized Training Pipeline:} Implement advanced data augmentation (Mixup, CutMix, random resized crops) and sampling strategies (Weighted Random Sampler) to combat severe class imbalance and prevent model overfitting.
    \item \textbf{Conduct a Comparative Analysis:} Systematically train and evaluate four deep learning architectures---EfficientNet-B0, ResNet50, DenseNet121, and Swin-Tiny---assessing their trade-offs in terms of accuracy, F1-score, computational efficiency, and AUC.
    \item \textbf{Ensure Model Interpretability:} Apply Grad-CAM to generate heatmaps that visualize the regions contributing to the models' decisions, ensuring alignment with dermatological features.
    \item \textbf{Perform Ablation Studies:} Isolate and quantify the performance contribution of individual regularization techniques to validate the proposed methodology.
    \item \textbf{Explore Ensemble Methods:} Combine predictions from all four architectures using soft voting to investigate whether architectural diversity leads to improved diagnostic performance.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Thesis Structure}

The remainder of this report is organized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Chapter 2 -- Literature Review:} Explores related work on deep learning for skin cancer classification.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 3 -- Methodology:} Details the dataset, architectures, optimization techniques, and evaluation metrics.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 4 -- Experiments and Results:} Presents comparative analysis, Grad-CAM interpretations, ensemble results, and ablation study findings.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 5 -- Conclusion and Future Work:} Summarizes contributions and proposes future directions.
\end{itemize}


% ======================= CHAPTER 2 ==========================
\chapter{Literature Review}

\section{Skin Lesion Classification with Deep Learning}

Deep learning for skin lesion classification changed significantly with the 2017 study by Esteva et al.~\cite{esteva2017dermatologist}, which trained an Inception-v3 CNN on 129,450 clinical images and compared it against 21 board-certified dermatologists on binary discrimination tasks. The network matched or exceeded dermatologist performance, establishing that transfer learning from large-scale pretraining datasets such as ImageNet~\cite{deng2009imagenet} could compensate for the comparatively small size of medical image collections. A broader confirmation followed: Maron et al.~\cite{maron2019systematic} found that CNNs consistently outperformed the median physician across 100,000 dermoscopy images and 112 participating dermatologists, though individual expert clinicians remained competitive.

Both of those studies addressed binary classification. The harder multi-class setting, which requires distinguishing among seven visually overlapping lesion categories, was formalised by the ISIC 2018 Challenge (Task 3)~\cite{codella2019skin}. Built from the HAM10000 collection~\cite{tschandl2018ham10000}, the challenge dataset covers Melanocytic Nevus (NV), Melanoma (MEL), Benign Keratosis (BKL), Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC), Actinic Keratosis (AKIEC), Vascular Lesion (VASC), and Dermatofibroma (DF). The difficulty is not only visual---MEL and NV share texture cues, and MEL and BKL share pigmentation patterns---but also statistical: NV accounts for roughly 67\% of training samples while DF and VASC together represent under 3\%~\cite{kassani2020comparative}. Any model evaluated on overall accuracy alone will appear to perform well on this dataset while almost entirely ignoring the minority classes.

\section{Evolution of Architectures}

Building an effective classifier for ISIC-style data requires choosing an architecture that transfers well from ImageNet despite the domain gap and remains trainable on the GPU budgets available to most research groups. The four architectures evaluated in this thesis each represent a distinct design philosophy, and understanding their differences helps explain the experimental results in Chapter~4.

ResNet~\cite{he2016deep} solved the fundamental problem of training very deep networks: without skip connections, gradients vanish before reaching early layers and learning stalls. By adding an identity shortcut around every pair of convolutional layers, He et al.\ allowed gradients to bypass non-linear transformations and reach earlier weights directly. The immediate practical effect was that 50-layer and 152-layer networks became trainable for the first time with stable convergence. Combined with batch normalisation~\cite{ioffe2015batch}, ResNet50 became the most commonly reported baseline in medical image classification, partly because its 25.6 million parameters provide sufficient capacity and partly because its behaviour under various training conditions is well understood.

DenseNet~\cite{huang2017densely} extended the skip-connection idea to its logical extreme: every layer receives concatenated feature maps from \textit{all} preceding layers within a dense block. The consequence for limited medical datasets is meaningful---each layer can access shallow edge detectors and mid-level texture descriptors without those features being progressively overwritten by subsequent convolutions. This reuse reduces the total number of parameters needed to reach a given accuracy level. DenseNet121 achieves competitive performance with roughly 8 million parameters, making it the most parameter-efficient CNN in this study.

EfficientNet~\cite{tan2019efficientnet} approached architecture design differently. Rather than handcrafting connectivity patterns, Tan and Le used neural architecture search to find a compact baseline block structure (B0) and then derived a compound scaling rule relating depth, width, and input resolution. The scaling rule specifies how to expand all three dimensions simultaneously as compute budget grows, avoiding the common failure mode of scaling only one dimension at the expense of the others. EfficientNet-B0, at 5.3 million parameters, achieves accuracy competitive with models several times its size. For clinical deployment contexts where inference may run on embedded hardware, this efficiency is practically significant.

Vision Transformers arrived in image classification with Dosovitskiy et al.~\cite{dosovitskiy2021image}, who divided an image into fixed-size patches, embedded each patch as a vector, and processed the sequence with self-attention. Self-attention has a structural advantage over convolution: every patch can attend to every other patch regardless of spatial distance, which matters when discriminative signals are spread across the image rather than localised. The practical problem is data scale---the original ViT required pre-training on JFT-300M, far beyond the reach of most medical imaging groups. DeiT~\cite{touvron2021deit} narrowed that gap through knowledge distillation, but Swin~\cite{liu2021swin} restructured the computation more fundamentally: attention is computed within local non-overlapping windows that shift between layers, recovering the hierarchical multi-scale representation of CNNs and reducing complexity from quadratic to linear in image size. Swin-Tiny, with 28 million parameters, is the largest model in this study.

The broader question of whether Transformers are inherently superior to CNNs was addressed directly by Liu et al.~\cite{liu2022convnext}, who applied Transformer-era training improvements (AdamW, cosine scheduling, strong augmentation) to a modernised ResNet structure and found that the resulting ConvNeXt matched Swin performance. The implication for this thesis is direct: if training methodology dominates architectural choice, then a well-regularised EfficientNet trained on a few thousand dermoscopy images may outperform a raw Swin Transformer---and the experiments in Chapter~4 show exactly this outcome.

\section{Handling Class Imbalance in Medical Datasets}

Buda et al.~\cite{buda2018systematic} ran a systematic comparison of class imbalance solutions across multiple medical imaging datasets and found that class-balanced mini-batches---achieved through oversampling or weighted sampling---consistently outperformed loss reweighting and undersampling. Weighted Random Sampling, which assigns each training sample a weight inversely proportional to its class frequency, implements this without discarding any data: every sample remains available for training, but minority-class samples are drawn more frequently in each mini-batch.

Loss function design provides a complementary lever. Focal Loss~\cite{lin2017focal} down-weights the contribution of confidently-classified examples, pushing gradient signal toward the hard borderline cases where rare and common classes look similar. Label Smoothing~\cite{szegedy2016rethinking} replaces one-hot targets with a soft distribution, preventing the model from assigning probability 1.0 to any class. Müller et al.~\cite{muller2019does} showed empirically that this calibration effect is most pronounced in datasets with structural class ambiguity---exactly the MEL/NV/BKL overlap that characterises ISIC 2018.

Data augmentation addresses imbalance from a third direction: generating additional effective training examples. Mixup~\cite{zhang2018mixup} linearly blends pairs of images and their labels to create virtual samples that lie between class boundaries, while CutMix~\cite{yun2019cutmix} achieves a related effect by pasting rectangular crops between images. Both techniques prevent the model from memorising specific minority-class examples, which is a real risk when Dermatofibroma has only 81 training images. Random Erasing~\cite{zhong2020random} masks rectangular input regions at random, acting as input-space dropout~\cite{srivastava2014dropout} and improving robustness to partial occlusions such as hair or dermoscope artefacts. A broad taxonomy of these and related techniques is provided by Shorten and Khoshgoftaar~\cite{shorten2019survey}.

\section{Explainability in Medical AI}

Clinical acceptance of an AI diagnostic tool depends on more than accuracy. A model that correctly identifies a melanoma must also provide reasoning that a clinician can examine---not because the model's internal computations are fully interpretable, but because a plausible attribution to the right image region is a meaningful sanity check. Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017grad} satisfies this requirement by computing, for a given prediction, the gradient of the target class score with respect to the last convolutional feature map and using those gradients to produce a channel-weighted spatial heatmap. For skin lesion classification specifically, Grad-CAM functions as a falsifiability test: a model that concentrates activation on the lesion interior and boundary is attending to features a dermatologist would recognise; one that lights up ruler markings or background skin is not, regardless of its test-set accuracy.

\section{Ensemble Methods in Medical Imaging}

Ensemble methods have consistently appeared in the top-performing ISIC challenge submissions~\cite{codella2019skin}, and the reason is straightforward: diverse models make errors in different places, and averaging their probability outputs cancels some of that error. Ganaie et al.~\cite{ganaie2022ensemble} surveyed the ensemble deep learning literature and found that soft voting---averaging raw probability vectors before taking the argmax---is competitive with more complex stacking strategies while being far simpler to implement. For medical applications, ensembles carry an additional benefit: the variance of the averaged probability vector signals prediction uncertainty, which is clinically meaningful. A case where all four models assign high probability to melanoma warrants different urgency than one where two predict melanoma and two predict nevus.

\section{Positioning This Work}

Top-performing entries in the original ISIC 2018 challenge reached balanced multi-class accuracies in the 85--88\% range~\cite{codella2019skin}, using large ensembles of ResNet and Inception variants, external training data, and extensive test-time augmentation. The present work makes a different trade-off: four architectures, a fixed training set, no external data, and single-pass inference---yet achieves $89.93 \pm 0.66\%$ accuracy and $0.982 \pm 0.001$ macro AUC when averaged across five random seeds. The individual models perform in line with challenge-era results; the gain comes from methodology. The ablation study in Chapter~4 shows that Mixup, CutMix, and weighted sampling together account for the bulk of the improvement over an unregularised baseline, suggesting that training recipe quality was the primary limiting factor for challenge-era systems rather than their choice of architecture.
