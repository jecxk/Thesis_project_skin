\section{Model Architectures}

Four architectures were selected to represent a diverse spectrum of modern deep learning approaches: three CNNs and one Vision Transformer. All models were initialized with ImageNet-pretrained weights~\cite{deng2009imagenet} through the \texttt{timm} library~\cite{wightman2019timm} and fine-tuned on the ISIC 2018 dataset.

\subsection{EfficientNet-B0}

EfficientNet-B0 is the baseline model of the EfficientNet family, designed using Neural Architecture Search (NAS) and compound scaling~\cite{tan2019efficientnet}. With only 5.3 million parameters, it achieves competitive accuracy by jointly optimizing network depth, width, and resolution. In our pipeline, a dropout layer~\cite{srivastava2014dropout} ($p=0.3$) was added before the final classification head.

\subsection{ResNet50}

ResNet50 consists of 50 layers organized into four residual stages~\cite{he2016deep}. Its skip connections enable effective gradient flow during backpropagation, making it one of the most reliable architectures for transfer learning. With 25.6 million parameters, it is the second-largest model in our comparison. Dropout ($p=0.3$) was applied before the classifier.

\subsection{DenseNet121}

DenseNet121 features 121 layers with dense connectivity, where each layer receives concatenated feature maps from all preceding layers within the same dense block~\cite{huang2017densely}. This architecture promotes feature reuse and reduces parameter count to approximately 8 million. Due to the inherent regularization provided by dense connections, a slightly lower dropout ($p=0.25$) was used.

\subsection{Swin-Tiny}

Swin-Tiny is a hierarchical Vision Transformer that processes images through shifted window-based self-attention~\cite{liu2021swin}. Unlike CNNs, which rely on local receptive fields, Swin captures long-range dependencies through attention mechanisms. With 28 million parameters, it is the largest model evaluated. Following best practices for Transformer fine-tuning~\cite{touvron2021deit}, a lower learning rate ($1 \times 10^{-4}$) and higher weight decay (0.05) were used, along with an extended warmup period of 5 epochs.

\section{Training Pipeline}

The training pipeline was built around one central problem: the 58:1 class imbalance. Four techniques address this from different angles---sampling, data augmentation, label target modification, and regularisation---and their individual contributions are quantified in the ablation study in Chapter~4.

\subsection{Weighted Random Sampling}

To counteract class imbalance during training, a Weighted Random Sampler was employed. Each sample is assigned a weight inversely proportional to the frequency of its class in the training set:

\begin{equation}
w_c = \frac{N}{C \cdot n_c}
\label{eq:class_weight}
\end{equation}

where $N$ is the total number of training samples, $C$ is the number of classes, and $n_c$ is the number of samples belonging to class $c$. This ensures that mini-batches are approximately class-balanced, preventing the model from developing a bias toward the majority class (NV).

\subsection{Mixup and CutMix Augmentation}

To further regularize training and improve generalization, Mixup and CutMix were applied with equal probability ($p=0.5$ each) during training.

\textbf{Mixup} creates virtual training examples by linearly interpolating pairs of images and their labels:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{x} = \lambda x_i + (1 - \lambda) x_j, \quad \tilde{y} = \lambda y_i + (1 - \lambda) y_j
\label{eq:mixup}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \alpha)$ with $\alpha = 0.2$.

\textbf{CutMix} replaces a rectangular patch of one image with the corresponding patch from another, adjusting the label proportionally to the area of the patch:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{x} = M \odot x_i + (1 - M) \odot x_j, \quad \tilde{y} = \lambda y_i + (1 - \lambda) y_j
\label{eq:cutmix}
\end{equation}
where $M$ is a binary mask indicating the cut region and $\lambda$ equals the ratio of the unmasked area. The CutMix alpha was set to 1.0.

Both techniques force the model to learn from ambiguous mixed signals. With only 81 Dermatofibroma training images, plain cross-entropy training memorises those examples within a few epochs; Mixup and CutMix prevent that by ensuring no training example has a hard one-hot label.

\subsection{Label Smoothing}

Standard one-hot encoded labels assign a probability of 1.0 to the correct class and 0.0 to all others. Label Smoothing redistributes a small fraction of the probability mass to incorrect classes:
\begin{equation}
y_{\text{smooth}} = (1 - \epsilon) \cdot y_{\text{one-hot}} + \frac{\epsilon}{C}
\label{eq:label_smooth}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon = 0.1$ is the smoothing factor and $C = 7$ is the number of classes. This prevents the model from assigning probability 1.0 to any class, which improves calibration and, as the ablation in Chapter~4 shows, macro F1-score---though at a small cost to AUC sharpness.

\subsection{Optimization and Scheduling}

All models were trained with AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019decoupled}, which separates weight decay from the adaptive gradient scaling. Standard Adam absorbs weight decay into its per-parameter learning rates, reducing its effective regularisation strength; AdamW applies it as a constant multiplicative penalty regardless of gradient history, which translates to better generalisation on held-out data. The learning rate follows a cosine annealing schedule with linear warmup~\cite{loshchilov2017sgdr}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Warmup phase} (first 3--5 epochs): The learning rate rises linearly from near zero to the target value. This prevents the randomly initialised classification head from producing large gradients that destabilise the pretrained feature extractor in the first few updates.
    \item \textbf{Cosine decay phase} (remaining epochs): The learning rate decreases along a cosine curve to a minimum of $1 \times 10^{-6}$, allowing the model to settle into a broad, flat region of the loss landscape rather than oscillating around a sharp minimum.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Data Augmentation}

Beyond Mixup and CutMix, the following standard augmentations were applied during training:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Random horizontal and vertical flips ($p=0.5$)
    \item Random rotation (up to $\pm90^\circ$)
    \item Color jittering (brightness, contrast, saturation, hue; magnitude 0.25)
    \item Random Resized Crop (scale range: 0.85--1.0)
    \item Gaussian Blur ($p=0.1$)
    \item Random Erasing ($p=0.1$)
\end{itemize}

All images were resized to $224 \times 224$ and normalised with ImageNet mean and standard deviation~\cite{deng2009imagenet}. Batch size was fixed at 16 for all models. This was dictated by the 28M-parameter Swin-Tiny under mixed-precision training; keeping the batch size identical across all architectures ensures that any performance differences reflect the models themselves rather than optimisation dynamics.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}

Accuracy alone is a misleading metric here: a classifier that predicts Melanocytic Nevus for every input achieves 67\% accuracy while detecting nothing clinically useful. The following metrics are therefore reported alongside accuracy:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Macro F1-Score:} The unweighted mean of per-class F1-scores. This is the primary model selection criterion because it gives equal weight to all seven classes regardless of how many samples each has.
    \item \textbf{Macro AUC:} The area under the one-vs-rest ROC curve, averaged across classes. AUC measures probability ranking quality independent of the decision threshold, making it sensitive to how well the model separates classes in continuous probability space.
    \item \textbf{Cohen's Kappa ($\kappa$):} Agreement between predicted and true labels corrected for the level of agreement expected by chance. Values above 0.6 are conventionally interpreted as substantial agreement.
    \item \textbf{Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC):} A single-number summary that accounts for all entries of the confusion matrix. Unlike F1, MCC penalises imbalanced predictions symmetrically across true positives, true negatives, false positives, and false negatives.
    \item \textbf{Per-class Sensitivity and Specificity:} Reported in confusion matrix form. In medical diagnostics, sensitivity (true positive rate) and specificity (true negative rate) carry different clinical costs: a missed melanoma is worse than a false alarm, so sensitivity for the MEL class is of particular interest.
\end{itemize}

\section{Ensemble Strategy}

After training all four models independently, a soft-voting ensemble was constructed by averaging the softmax probability vectors element-wise across models and taking the class with the highest averaged probability:

\begin{equation}
\hat{y} = \arg\max_c \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^{M} p_m(c \mid x)
\label{eq:ensemble}
\end{equation}

where $M=4$ is the number of models and $p_m(c \mid x)$ is the probability assigned to class $c$ by model $m$. No additional weights or learned combination are used---the four models are treated as equally reliable. The motivation is that models with different inductive biases (local convolutional kernels versus global window attention) make errors in different regions of the input space, and simple averaging cancels some of that error without requiring a held-out set for weight fitting.

\section{Model Interpretability with Grad-CAM}

To verify that the trained models attend to the lesion rather than background artefacts, Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017grad} was applied to all four architectures on held-out test images. Given a target class $c$, Grad-CAM computes the gradient of the class score $y^c$ with respect to the feature maps $A^k$ of the target layer:

\begin{equation}
\alpha_k^c = \frac{1}{Z} \sum_i \sum_j \frac{\partial y^c}{\partial A_{ij}^k}
\label{eq:gradcam_weights}
\end{equation}

The final heatmap is obtained by computing a weighted combination of the feature maps followed by a ReLU activation:

\begin{equation}
L_{\text{Grad-CAM}}^c = \text{ReLU}\left(\sum_k \alpha_k^c A^k\right)
\label{eq:gradcam}
\end{equation}

This produces a coarse localization map highlighting regions that positively influence the prediction for class $c$. The heatmap is then upsampled to the input image resolution and overlaid as a color-coded visualization.
